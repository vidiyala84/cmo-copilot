The problem — in plain terms

You run the ads for a growing brand.

You're spending over a million dollars a month, spread across hundreds of campaigns. Every week the results move — some up, some down — and you have to decide where the money should go next.

Why it's genuinely hard

One campaign's sales suddenly drop to almost zero.

The obvious move
Shut it off. Move the money elsewhere.
The truth
The sales didn't stop — a tracking tag broke. The campaign is fine.

Kill it, and you've thrown away a winner. The right answer is sometimes to do nothing — and that's the hardest call for a human, or an AI, to get right. The obvious move is often the wrong one.

The tempting shortcut

I could just hand-code the exceptions.

Write a rule for each trap: "if sales drop but clicks are steady, it's tracking — hold." It works… until you notice I had to know every trap in advance.

Real businesses meet new traps constantly, and hard-coded rules quietly overfit to the past. Which raised the actual question…

The breakthrough

A memory that learns the exceptions — from experience.

a decision → its real outcome
    → learn the precise condition that caused it
    → keep it only if it truly mattered
    → apply it automatically, next time

When a decision backfires, the copilot learns the exact condition behind it — from its own history — and applies it on its own from then on. Nobody writes the rules. It writes them, and only keeps the ones its history proves it needed.

It works — and it holds

From unreliable to reliable — and it stays there.
